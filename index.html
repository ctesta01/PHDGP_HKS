The Original PHDGP

The techniques involved in using geographic data in health analyses have come a long way, both in part due to the work by Nancy Krieger in the original Public Health Disparities Geocoding Project, and due to maturation of statistical techniques and geographic information systems (or GIS) software.

The original PHDGP was born out of Nancy’s prior work analyzing Cancer Registry data in relation to residential addresses. She was an early pioneer in this field, literally geocoding addresses to census tracts by hand, but certainly not the first.

The first incarnation of PHDGP aimed not just to inform public health practitioners as to how to go about their work assessing, documenting, and combatting health disparities, but to also ask the question: at what geographic level, and using what social variables, are health disparities best understood? That work culminated in recommendations to focus health disparities research in the US at the Census Tract level and to use the proportion of households under the federal poverty line as defined by cutpoints ranging from 0-5%, 5-10%, 10-20%, and 20% and above.

Origins of the Ideas

Projects tying the health of the people to their geographical conditions have their roots in the earliest examples of epidemiological studies.

This kind of work is only made possible through use of geographic data describing the population — including cadastral maps and Census data tables.

In 1840, Louis-René Villermé studied the living and working conditions of factory workers in various industries. He found that poor living and working conditions were directly correlated with higher mortality rates. This was, of course, contrary to the prevalent theories of disease distribution at the time, which included miasma theory (literally connoting “bad air”), moral failures and behavior of the poor, and divine punishment.

Health Equity

Health equity, one of the central goals of Public Health, refers to a conditions in which everyone has a fair and just opportunity to be as healthy as possible.

Unfortunately we live in a world where today there are inequities in health that are unfair, unjust and preventable.

These inequitable conditions have been created by a long history of discrimination and marginalization. Highlighted here are the detectable effects of Jim Crow laws and historical redlining which kept People of Color from housing in more White, affluent neighborhoods across the United States and served to concentrate poverty and People of Color in areas that were given lower grades as a result of their proximity to industrial sources of pollution, less than ideal living conditions, and other characteristics that were perceived as undesirable.

Health Justice

Similar, but distinct to the concept of health equity is the concept of health justice.

Health justice is an approach to public health that seeks to eliminate health disparities and achieve equity by dismantling systems of discrimination, oppression, and marginalization that contribute to poor health outcomes.

Health justice emphasizes the importance of addressing the root causes of health inequities and redistributing power away from unjust systems to ensure that all individuals and communities have access to the resources, opportunities, and conditions necessary to achieve optimal health and well-being.

This means challenging and and dismantling institutional and structural barriers that perpetuate health disparities, including racism, gender and sexuality based discrimination, ableism, weight stigma, ageism, and others.

In particular, while “Health Equity” has become a bit of a trendy term popularly invoked to garner support for varied causes, the popularity of “Health Justice,” which directly asks from whom power must be wrested and how resources must be redistributed to create equitable conditions, has, in contrast, lagged.

Ecosocial Theory

With that, I want to introduce the Ecosocial Theory of Disease Distribution which is a framework for understanding health disparities that Nancy Krieger has been developing for the past several decades.

Ecosocial theory specifically asks us to identify how individuals physically and biologically embody the harms, disadvantages and injustices done unto them by systems of oppression that exist as a result of contemporary political economy across the life-course and across levels of society.

Moreover, this framework calls into focus that the accountability of systems of power and individuals’ agency within them are foundational causal factors in the explanation of the inequitable distribution of disease.

Methods

Geocoding

It’s in the name: Geocoding — so what is it?

Geocoding refers to the process by which we link health records or really any kind of records that exist in a dataset to geographic entities.

This is such an incredibly powerful technique because in health systems data entry is often a pain-point: Many doctors complain about how entering data into the Electronic Medical Record means that’s often time they spend typing instead of facing the patient and actually seeing them eye-to-eye.

One field that huge numbers of databases and data systems routinely collect is individuals’ residential addresses. With that information, we can use services like Google Maps, ArcGIS, and the Census Geocoder to convert those into latitude and longitude locations that can then be easily linked to geographic areas.

Area-Based Social Metrics (ABSMs) are quantitative measures that capture and represent the social, economic, and demographic characteristics of a specific geographic area or community.

  • Housing and living conditions;
  • Access to healthcare services including density of healthcare facilities and insurance coverage
  • Environmental factors like air and water quality, access to green spaces, and environmental hazards

The Index of Concentration at the Extremes

The Index of Concentration at the Extremes (ICE) measures the extent to which a population in a specified area is concentrated into best-off and worst-off extremes of a specified social distribution.

The ICE was introduced into the sociological literature by Massey in 2001 to measure economic polarization.

Building on Massey’s work, in 2015 Krieger et al introduced two new novel ICE measures, one for racialized segregation, and one for racialized economic segregation.

Consider what the ICE for racialized economic segregation might look like at different geographic levels – namely the county, congressional district, and state levels.

The Modifiable Areal Unit Problem and Ecological Fallacy

Figure from https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modifiable_areal_unit_problem#/media/File:Maup_rate_numbers.png

Figure from https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Simpsons_paradox_-_animation.gif

In fact, in geography and spatial statistics, this problem is well understood and comes in two flavors:

The modifiable areal unit problem states that if one chooses to change what boundaries are used, they can change what story the data tell — and of course this is well understood by those who work in the gerrymandering space as one example.

Another flavor of this dimension to spatial data analysis is the ecological fallacy. The ecological fallacy is the mistaken idea that correlations drawn at a geographically aggregate level can be applied to smaller geographical levels, particularly the individual level.

An easy to understand example of this is what while states that are more wealthy tend to vote more democratically, in many states individuals with higher income tend to vote more conservatively, demonstrating the principle that correlations drawn at the state level can’t be assumed to hold at the individual level.

The relevance to the figure on the right is that we can imagine collapsing each of these strata of observations into an average for each strata, which if we perform a regression analysis on, we would see the decreasing relationship between X and Y. On the other hand, if we are able to analyze the data within strata at the individual data-point level, we see a different, increasing relationship. This is something that can happen when we aggregate observations up to an area-level and draw correlations, so it’s important to be mindful of the potential for this to happen.

Age Standardization

Available from https://seer.cancer.gov/stdpopulations/stdpop.19ages.html

People run into two incredibly common problems with maps of health outcomes: The first being the failure to use a denominator so we often can’t differentiate what are the effects of population distribution vs. other factors, and the second is failure to adjust for important variations in age distribution.

In a setting like the greater Boston area, one can immediately see why this would be so important! We have so many census tracts that are dominated by college and university dormitories where the median age is just slightly over 20. If we were looking at, for example, COVID-19 mortality rates by census tract, it should be no surprise that we’d see very low mortality rates in such census tracts given that higher age is one of the single biggest contributors to COVID-19 mortality risk.

So we have to do something about that before we let our audiences loose on maps where area level rates are visualized. The appropriate choice is to age standardize the rates shown in the map. This is done by re-weighting the age-strata-specific rates according to the age-breakdown of an agreed-upon standardized reference population.

Aggregate Methods

In the figure on the left is shown the areas with the highest poverty rates in red where 20-100% of households are in poverty, and in the lightest shade are households in the lowest poverty rate areas, where less than 5% of households are under the federal poverty line.

A typical health disparities research question might be what is the ratio of premature mortality rates in the highest poverty areas to the lowest poverty areas. And here premature mortality is defined as death before the age of 65.

In the simplest method, we can sum up the numbers of premature deaths in the census tracts from each poverty level, the amount of person-time during which these observations could have occurred (i.e., the number of people who could have died multiplied by the duration of observation-time), and we could calculate rates of premature mortality per 100,000 person-years as a crude rate.

But this wouldn’t account for if there’s a systematic difference in age between the poverty levels — it could happen that some of these poverty levels are younger or older than the rest, potentially affecting what we’d see in the crude rates.

Instead, we use the Year 2000 Standard Million age distribution to re-weight the age specific rates in order to calculate age standardized rates. Again, this is like controlling for age and saying “what rate would we have observed if the age distribution had been as in the Year 2000 Standard Million?”

One we have our age-standardized rates, we can look at those and see that there’s a roughly 2.15x rate ratio in premature mortality between the highest poverty areas and lowest poverty areas.

Non-Spatial Regression

  • Modern epidemiologists and statisticians might be inclined to take a regression approach to estimation of inequities.
  • This permits statistical adjustment for multiple covariates (not just age)
  • Typically we model age-specific disease rates using a log-linear Poisson model as follows: \[Y_{ij} \sim \text{Poisson}(\mu_{ij})\] \[\text{log}(\mu_{ij}) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + ... + \beta_p x_p + \log(n_{ij})\] where \(Y_{ij}\) is the observed count in area \(i\), covariate stratum \(j\) and \(n_{ij}\) is the population person-time that gave rise to the observed count.
  • This yields \(\hat \beta\) estimates that are interpretable as log incidence rate ratios (exponentiate to transform them back to the rate ratio scale).

The aggregate method does satisfy some needs, and we can perform age standardization with it, but things would certainly get tricky if we wanted to incorporate multiple area based social metrics.

Instead, a more comprehensive framework is to use regression.

What’s crucial in epidemiology when we’re dealing with discrete health outcome events is that we use a count-based model.

We again have to be careful to specify the amount of person-time during which events can occur.

These will allow us to adjust for age, modeling rate ratios for each age strata.

I just want to point out that Poisson regression models are a bit of an old stand-by, but there are others models that more flexibly allow for overdispersion, namely the Quasi-Poisson and Negative Binomial models, but I don’t think I have time today to get into those.

This is a nice approach for understanding systematic variations and co-variations between area-based social metrics, risk factors, and health outcomes.

Small Area Estimation

Besag, York and Mollié (BYM) recommend combining a conditional-autoregressive (CAR) prior and a standard normal prior to allow for both:

  • spatially unstructured latent covariates, and
  • spatially correlated latent covariates \[O_i \sim \text{Poisson}(\theta_i E_i)\] \[\text{log}(\theta_i) = \beta_0 + U_i + V_i\] \[V_i \sim \text{Normal}(0, \sigma_V^2)\] \[U_i | U_{j, j \neq i} \sim \text{Normal}(\mu_i, \sigma_U^2 / n_i)\] where \(\mu_i\) is the average among neighbor regions to region \(i\) and \(n_i\) is the population size of regions neighboring region \(i\).

One of the sources of variation we haven’t yet addressed is spatial autocorrelation, or the tendencies of neighboring areas’ outcomes to be correlated.

There are many models that account for spatial autocorrelation including those proposed by Besag, York and Mollié, the Leroux model, and others — but the idea central to all of them is that we need to be explicit about modeling spatial structure and spatial interdependence in our data.

Here, the BYM model is saying that the area level rates should be a function of the average of their neighboring area’s rates, randomness, and other predictors.

Spatial and Territorial Stigmatization

  • It is critical that we contextualize health inequities with analyses of social, political, and ecological context.
  • Observations of health disparities do not necessarily translate to common understandings of cause.
  • Without offering explanatory context of resources and hazards, presentations of health disparities can perpetuate harmful ideas and actions that undermine the elimination of health inequities; such presentations can create or reinforce territorial stigmatization where places are falsely associated with the moral character and behavior of its residents.
  • If some places are found to have a high concentration of illness or disease, narratives and representations of those places as “diseased,” “contaminated,” could produce or reinforce existing stigma and lead to counter-productive targeted interventions such as heightened policing and surveillance in an attempt to contain and control residents, reclamation or demolition of physical structures, and social neglect and abandonment.

A Research Framework

I want to conclude with this slide from Nancy Krieger.

As you will see, Social Justice and Public Health are twin pillars of ecosocial theory.

The point of analyzing how injustices such as structural racism or any other harm health is not to prove that they are wrong, since they are definitionally unjust, but rather to do correct science and to generate publicly testable and tested actionable knowledge about who is bearing the burden of exposure to disease, illness, and death.

The point is not to do so-called politically correct science, but rather to do correct science that rigorously addresses the causal mechanisms, complex social pathways, and spatiotemporal realities of how health inequities are perpetuated and, conversely, how they can be ended.

We hope that you will take advantage of the resources we have collated for you, and that you will continue asking the hard questions of who must do what, in collaboration with whom, in order to advance health justice given the new knowledge that can be generated using the methods presented here and otherwise.
